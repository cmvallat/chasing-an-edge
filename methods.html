Speed Bumps: Why It’s So Hard to Catch Cheaters in Track and Field

A cache of leaked blood tests showed hundreds of track athletes have recorded results “suggestive of doping.” Take a look at why anti-doping tests are so ineffective.

by David Epstein, Aug. 20, 2015, 8 a.m. EDT

Earlier this month, London’s Sunday Times and German broadcaster ARD published a joint investigation on doping in track and field that included an analysis of 12,000 leaked blood tests from 5,000 athletes between 2001 and 2012. The tests had been carried out by the IAAF, track and field’s international governing body. Two respected experts in doping methods said blood tests of 800 of the athletes were “highly suggestive of doping or at the very least abnormal.” Ten runners who won medals in endurance events at the 2012 Summer Olympics in London had suspicious test results. And a startling 80 percent of Russian medalists recorded tests that showed likely doping. The vast majority of athletes with suspicious tests were never sanctioned.

On Saturday, the 2015 track and field world championships kick off and, of course, some athletes who are doping will vie for medals. Most will not be caught; only 1 to 2 percent of tests in international Olympic sports result in sanctions each year. If doping is so rife in track and field, why are athletes penalized so rarely? It’s partly because many suspicious tests don’t quite reach the high evidence bar to be considered officially positive. But it’s also because doping athletes tend to employ methods that make drug testing extremely difficult. As Paul Scott, head of Scott Analytics, which provides testing services in multiple sports has put it: “Drug testing has a public reputation that far exceeds its capabilities.”

With most drug tests, such as testosterone, there is a range of acceptable values due to variation in athlete’s natural hormones. The upper limit of this range is usually set at a very high bar so as to avoid false positive tests. Thus, some athletes take just a little bit of illegal substance so as to gain a marginal advantage while still staying within the limits of the test. In races that are determined by hundredths of a second, microdosing can provide a critical advantage while still avoiding detection.

In this case, an athlete has to get a Therapeutic Use Exemption, or TUE, which are legal “passes” for otherwise illegal medicines or hormones. However, some cheaters try to obtain TUEs by lying about medical conditions.

Testing agencies tell athletes to be available for a test within a scheduled window of a few hours on a certain date; these restrictions are known as “whereabouts requirements.” However, if an athlete misses a test, they can give plausible deniability that they weren’t available due to an “emergency” without punishment, up to three times. So, some cheating athletes can just avoid a test if they know they have drugs in their system and give a random excuse for why they missed a test.

Carbon Isotope Ratio (CIR) testing is a way to distinguish between natural and synthetic testosterone, which is a common hormone that athletes use to dope. However, this test is laborious and costly, and usually only used to follow up on a previous suspicious test. Also, CIR testing is not sensitive enough to detect synthetic testosterone at low concentrations, so athletes can still microdose.

Another method of testing is the Athlete Biological Passport, which detects fluctuations in an individual athlete’s body over time. This establishes a baseline for minimum and maximum hormones for each individual, so the range of “acceptable values” can be limited to an athlete’s historical hormone levels, thereby making microdosers more likely to be caught.

Testing athletes repeatedly, outside of competition, and without advanced notice would keep cheating athletes on their toes. Also, testing agencies have started saving testing samples for 10 years to recheck when new, more effective testing methods are developed. That way, one can see if an athlete had been cheating in previous years, but avoided tests due to faults in technology. Additionally, more stringent TUE passes and whereabouts requirements would make it harder for athletes to obtain illegal drugs for medical reasons, and to have to adhere to a stricter testing schedule.
